## The four-axis analysis

### Palette
- **Two tones + one loud accent.** Off-white, charcoal, and a single lime green.
- Palette discipline is absolute — the lime appears only on interactive moments and one or two brand payoff beats. Every other surface is restricted.
- **Why it works:** restraint compounds. When every surface is quiet, the single loud note becomes an event. Tailwind-default sites dilute accent color across dozens of surfaces; OFF+BRAND reserve it like ammunition.
- **Staling risk:** "two-tone + neon accent" is being copied widely post-SOTD. PRO should derive the accent from the subject, not from a trend.

### Layout
- **Chromatic section shifts.** Each major section swaps the palette — race section goes lime-on-black, portrait section goes charcoal-on-cream, career section goes silver-on-navy. Scroll feels like flipping magazine chapters.
- **Typography-as-interface.** Helmet labels, lap counters, and car numbers are interactive type — no UI chrome, just type that responds.
- **Viewport-locked sections.** Each chapter fills a full viewport; transitions are deliberate.
- **Why it works:** the subject is an F1 driver — inherently chaptered (races, seasons, teams). The layout mirrors the subject's structure.
- **Staling risk:** chromatic shifts are trendy. PRO applies this when the subject is genuinely chaptered, not as a default.

### Motion
- **Scroll-scrubbed cinematic.** Every major reveal is scroll-linked, not time-linked. Reader paces the film.
- **Subtle reactive hover states.** Helmet labels tilt a half-degree on hover; lap counters animate on reveal; car numbers count up.
- Motion category: **narrative + reactive.** The scroll is the film. The reactive states are feedback.
- **Why it works:** matches the F1 subject (cinematic, precise, controlled).
- **Staling risk:** generic "F1-style cinematic" scroll — avoid for non-F1 subjects.

### Type
- Condensed grotesque for civic labels (lap times, race numbers)
- Editorial serif for biographical copy
- Monospace for technical specs
- **Why it works:** the faces match F1's information hierarchy — public-facing (grotesque) + personal (serif) + technical (mono).

## What PRO carries forward

1. **Extreme color restraint.** Two non-colors + one loud accent, with the accent reserved for load-bearing moments only.
2. **Chromatic section shifts** when the subject is genuinely chaptered. Each major section gets its own palette, shifted from the previous — scroll feels like page-turning.
3. **Typography-as-interface.** Remove UI chrome wherever possible; make the type itself interactive.
4. **Scroll-scrubbed cinematic narrative** for subjects with natural temporal structure (races, case studies, timelines).

## What PRO does NOT carry forward

1. **The F1 aesthetic specifically.** Don't produce "racing-style" pages for non-racing subjects.
2. **Lime green as an accent.** It's Lando's; the specific hue is subject-derived, not portable.
3. **Helmet-label hover patterns.** Subject-specific; the typography-as-interface principle carries forward but not the exact implementation.

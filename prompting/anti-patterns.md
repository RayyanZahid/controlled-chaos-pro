# Anti-patterns: what kills a brief

Eight common ways briefs fail. Each has a specific tell. Most are fixable in two minutes.

## 1. The adjective pile

> *"Modern, clean, bold, innovative, approachable, human."*

Six adjectives, zero nouns, no stance. Every one is a category the model has resolved toward *generic* a thousand times in training. Strip them and ask: *what specific thing do you actually mean?* **"Modern"** means almost nothing. **"A 2026 editorial rather than a 1998 brochure"** means something.

## 2. The menu pick

> *"Brutalist mode with the blaze palette, kinetic voice."*

Picking from any design-system taxonomy (CC v1's or anyone else's) is exactly what PRO exists to replace. The output becomes a lookup. The whole point of the skill is that the palette, voice, and mode derive from the subject — when you menu-pick, you strip the subject out of the equation before the skill has a chance to read it.

## 3. The pre-baked solution

> *"Make it look like Stripe."* / *"Make it like Linear."*

This isn't a brief — it's a copy-paste instruction. Worse, *"make it like Stripe"* tells the model nothing about *your* subject. Stripe's palette works for Stripe. What is yours about? Derive from there.

## 4. The 20-page kitchen sink

Briefs that bury the proposition under 18 pages of context, appendices, persona charts, and stakeholder notes. Howard Gossage's rule applies: *"How often do you need to be told your house is on fire?"* One page. One proposition. If the core idea can't survive compression to a paragraph, it isn't the core idea yet.

## 5. The committee brief

Three stakeholders signed off; none of them agree; the brief reads as a truce, not a thesis. You can hear it in the phrasing — *"we need to balance X and Y while also acknowledging..."* Pick one side. A brief written to placate management is not a brief.

## 6. The goal-execution blur

> *"We need a minimal landing page with a hero, three features, and a CTA."*

This isn't a brief — it's a wireframe. The proposition is missing. What is the page arguing for? Why should anyone care? Strip out every execution detail and ask again: *what is the point?*

## 7. The spec sheet for a poem

Using bullet-point structure for a creative task:

- Warm palette
- Editorial voice
- Scroll reveals
- Typography-as-interface

Technically unambiguous; creatively dead. The format itself has told the model you want a checklist. Anthropic's own guidance: the prompt register shapes the output register. Prose in, prose out. Bullets in, bullets out.

## 8. The vague gesture

> *"Something beautiful about memory."* / *"Something emotional but tasteful."*

The opposite of #1-7. No structure, no specificity, no nouns, no anchors. The model hallucinates into the vacuum and returns whatever its training average says about *memory* or *emotion*. Generic, predictably.

---

## The read-aloud test

Read your brief out loud. If it sounds like a memo or a checklist, rewrite. If it sounds like the first paragraph of an essay someone would actually want to read, it is ready.

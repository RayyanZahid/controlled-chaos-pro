# Exemplars: paired briefs

Ten briefs. Five bad-vs-good pairs, one per persona. The bad versions are common. The good versions generate.

---

## Saville — source-before-style

**Bad**
> *"A tribute page for Factory Records. Moody palette, editorial typography, good scroll motion."*

**Good**
> *"A tribute to Peter Saville, for the sleeve that invented the method. Fantin-Latour's basket of roses — Saville saw the painting at the National Gallery and used it without treatment because the flowers already meant what the record was about. Cream sleeve-stock, dusty rose, deep stem-green. 1983 typography — Neue Haas Grotesk and Bembo. The page should read like the inner gatefold of a record sleeve."*

**Why the second generates:** it names a specific painting, a specific year, a specific typeface pair, a specific stance (*"tribute to the method"*), and a specific output analog (*"inner gatefold of a record sleeve"*). Four anchors. The model has everything it needs.

---

## Scher — scale-as-voice

**Bad**
> *"A manifesto page about principles-based design. Bold typography, high contrast."*

**Good**
> *"Three civic-scale beats, each filling the viewport — 'DERIVE, DON'T DECORATE.' 'NO TEMPLATES.' 'EVERY PAGE IS ONE OF ONE.' Between them, dense counterweight: the axioms and personas listed small and tight like a poster's fine print. Two-tone with one loud note — the palette restraint of a Paula Scher Public Theater poster, nothing more."*

**Why the second generates:** three phrases you could tattoo, one layout instruction (*"civic-scale beats fill the viewport"*), one counterweight rule (*"dense and small"*), and one reference object (*"Public Theater poster"*). The declarative structure mirrors what the output should do.

---

## Rams — pay-rent test

**Bad**
> *"Clean minimalist product page for a design tool. Functional, elegant, no fluff."*

**Good**
> *"A product page for a CLI tool, built like a Braun T3 pocket radio. One typeface. Three sizes. No hero image. No ambient motion. If an element doesn't help a reader decide whether to install the tool, remove it. One italic sentence at the end that admits, without apology, that the page looks this quiet because the method demanded it."*

**Why the second generates:** a specific industrial-design analog (*"Braun T3 pocket radio"*), three counting constraints (*"one / three / no"*), a subtractive test in plain language (*"if it doesn't help… remove it"*), and a pre-planned discord element (*"one italic sentence at the end"*).

---

## Weingart — disciplined rebellion

**Bad**
> *"Experimental typography zine, post-modern, layered type, chaotic."*

**Good**
> *"A fictional quarterly called Typografische. Issue nine. Rigorous 12-column Swiss grid across the masthead and every article column. ONE full-bleed spread where the grid breaks — layered type, rotation, chromatic aberration in cyan and magenta printer-ink off-register. Basel School, 1972, but in a browser. The violation must be legible as intentional, not mistakable for a render bug."*

**Why the second generates:** the underlying grid is named (*"12-column Swiss"*), the violation is located (*"ONE spread"*), the aesthetic lineage is anchored (*"Basel School, 1972"*), and the primary failure mode is pre-empted (*"not mistakable for a render bug"*).

---

## Bierut — adaptive reuse

**Bad**
> *"Rebrand page for a design system. Show the transformation. Bold, clean, clear."*

**Good**
> *"A page documenting v1 → PRO as a Bierut-style adaptive reuse. Heritage swatch at the top showing v1's actual named palettes — void, blaze, dusk, earth, concrete — as small flat tiles. That swatch is the discord; everything else on the page is cooled and transformed from those same colors. Then a wordmark with v1 struck through and PRO in the new accent. Four transformation sections: what we audited, what we kept, what we transformed, what we removed. Palette desaturated and cooled from v1's amber and charcoal. Scroll reveals the transformation step by step."*

**Why the second generates:** a named method (*"Bierut-style adaptive reuse"*), a specific structural move located (*"heritage swatch at the top, that swatch is the discord"*), a palette transformation described in terms of its source (*"desaturated and cooled from v1's amber and charcoal"*), and a narrative sequence specified (*"audited / kept / transformed / removed"*).

---

## The pattern across all five

Every good brief above has the same DNA:

1. **A named reference object** the model can latch onto — a painting, a radio, a poster, a grid school, a palette inheritance
2. **A stance or relation** to something known — tribute, rivalry, violation, descent
3. **At least one sensory or structural anchor** — cream stock, civic-scale, three sizes, 12-column grid, heritage swatch
4. **Not one adjective pile** — concrete, not categorical

Steal the move, not the brief.

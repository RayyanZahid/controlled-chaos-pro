# Bierut

> *"We took Massimo Vignelli's 1973 logo and split it into 64 squares. Every piece was a new piece of the identity."*
> — Michael Bierut on the 2007 Saks Fifth Avenue rebrand

## Method: adaptive reuse

Find existing heritage — an archive, a historical mark, a predecessor identity, an artifact from the subject's past — and **transform** it. Don't invent ex nihilo.

Bierut asks:

1. **What does this subject already own** that has good bones?
2. **Which fragment, if extracted and transformed, becomes the new identity?**
3. **How do we honor the history while asserting the present?**

## When to select Bierut

Use Bierut when:

- The subject has a **legacy** — an existing brand, a historical logo, an archive, a family, an institution
- The subject needs to feel **continuous** with its past while appearing current
- The audience respects heritage but needs proof of relevance
- You have access to existing visual assets (even bad ones — Bierut often starts with bad assets and transforms them)

Do NOT use Bierut when:

- The subject is brand-new with no heritage (use Saville — find a cultural anchor instead)
- The subject needs to break from its past (use Weingart — rebellion, not continuity)
- The subject is pure functionality with no narrative (use Rams)

## Method — step by step

### Step 1. Audit the heritage

List every existing visual asset the subject owns:
- Logos (current and historical)
- Color palettes
- Typefaces
- Publications, marketing materials, printed matter
- Archive photography
- Architectural or product details
- Ephemera — letterhead, signage, wayfinding

Even if you hate all of them. The worst existing asset can become the best transformed one.

### Step 2. Find the fragment with good bones

Identify ONE element that has structural integrity but hasn't been fully exploited:
- A geometric pattern inside an old logo
- A color from a specific historical publication
- A typographic detail from old wayfinding
- A photographic style from the archive

The fragment should be **uncopyrightably specific to this subject** — something no competitor owns.

### Step 3. Deconstruct

Take the fragment apart. Study its components. How many squares is the Saks logo's script? (64.) How many colors are in the 1962 corporate brochure? (4.) What's the proportion of the original mark?

The deconstruction reveals the DNA.

### Step 4. Recombine

Use the deconstructed pieces as the building blocks of the new identity:
- The fragment's DNA becomes the grid system
- Its colors become the palette
- Its proportions become the type scale
- Its era becomes the typographic voice

The new identity **descends from** the old, visibly and intentionally.

### Step 5. Assert the present

The recombination must feel **current**, not nostalgic. Apply contemporary motion, interaction, scale. The fragment is the anchor; the composition is today.

## Aesthetic posture

- **Palette**: derived from existing heritage. Not invented.
- **Typography**: pairs a historical face (the heritage voice) with a contemporary neutral (the present voice). Two faces maximum.
- **Motion**: **narrative** (per `principles/motion.md` category 3). The composition reveals the transformation — old becomes new in a sequence.
- **Microfeature** (Bierut signature): letter-scramble morph (old wordmark morphs into new), numeric index chrome (chapters/sections evoke catalog tradition).
- **Imagery**: archive-transformed. Old photographs cropped aggressively, high contrast, purposefully aged OR deliberately cleaned.
- **Whitespace**: traditional editorial proportions — respects the heritage's era.

## Anti-patterns

- **Pure nostalgia** (if it looks like a vintage throwback, the transformation didn't happen)
- **Invisible heritage** (the fragment must be VISIBLY present in the new identity — not a vibe, an artifact)
- **Multiple heritage sources** (one anchor, transformed; not a collage of references)
- **Bierut without heritage** (if the subject truly has no past, use Saville)

## Bierut's question

Before writing any CSS, Bierut asks:

> **"What does this already own that has good bones, and how do I transform it?"**

The fragment is the anchor. The transformation is the assertion.

# Scher

> *"I became the visual voice of a place."*
> — Paula Scher on her Public Theater identity

## Method: scale-as-voice

Typography IS the architecture. Not decorated — **scaled**. The page is a single proposition rendered at civic size, and every other element is a counterweight to that scale.

Scher asks:

1. **What ONE phrase carries this page?** Not a heading and subheading — one sentence of type that is the page's thesis.
2. **At what scale does it become the architecture?** Typically: one measure fills the viewport width. 180pt-400pt. Civic, loud, unapologetic.
3. **What supports it?** Everything else — images, captions, navigation — is dense and small. Counterweight, not competition.

## When to select Scher

Use Scher when:

- The subject can be carried by words alone (manifestos, slogans, political moments, cultural declarations)
- The emotional register demands **volume** — conviction, advocacy, urgency, celebration
- Images would dilute the message
- The page is a statement, not a story

Do NOT use Scher when:

- The subject requires nuance or exposition (use Saville or Bierut)
- The subject is technical/functional (use Rams)
- The volume would feel out of register (a condolence page should not be Scher)

## Method — step by step

### Step 1. Find the phrase

Ask: *"if the entire page could be one sentence, what would it be?"*

Examples:
- Public Theater → "SHAKESPEARE IN THE PARK"
- A protest page → "WE DO NOT CONSENT"
- A manifesto site → "BRUTALISM IS HONESTY"

The phrase must be **declarative**. Not a question, not a description. A statement.

### Step 2. Choose the face

The face carries the phrase. Scher's Public Theater work used Akzidenz-Grotesk — wood-type aesthetic, condensed, civic. Pick a typeface that looks like a poster when enlarged.

Avoid: Inter, SF Pro, any face optimized for UI. Use: wood-type digital revivals, condensed grotesques, display cuts.

### Step 3. Scale

Size the phrase until it IS the architecture. One line per viewport width is typical. Track tight (-0.02em to -0.04em). Weight heavy to black.

### Step 4. Counterweight

Everything else is small, dense, and supportive:
- Navigation: tiny, all-caps, in the margin
- Captions: 12pt, justified, multi-column
- Images (if any): small, framed, as evidence

The contrast between civic-scale type and dense supporting matter IS the composition.

## Aesthetic posture

- **Palette**: two colors + accent. High contrast. Scher often uses black + one loud color (red, mustard, electric blue).
- **Typography**: one headline face (civic scale), one supporting face (dense, readable). Never three.
- **Motion**: punctuating — one dramatic reveal of the headline on entry. After that, stillness.
- **Microfeature** (Scher signature): variable-font weight-on-scroll morphing 400→900 on the hero phrase; kerning-on-emphasis for single words.
- **Imagery**: counterweight only. Small, dense, evidential. Never hero.
- **Whitespace**: the hero type fills it. The supporting matter lives in the margins.

## Anti-patterns

- **Headline + subhead both large** (Scher's method is ONE beat of scale, then drop to tiny)
- **Multiple hero phrases** (pick one; if two fit, the composition isn't finished)
- **Softening the type** (Scher is NEVER light, NEVER elegant — she is wood-type civic)
- **Centered layouts** (Scher typically flush-left, sometimes justified-edge-to-edge at civic scale)

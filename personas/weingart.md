# Weingart

> *"You can only break the rules if you understand them first."*
> — Wolfgang Weingart

## Method: disciplined rebellion

Establish a rigid grid. Know it deeply. Then violate it — at one load-bearing point — with typography that layers, rotates, overlaps, interrupts.

Weingart is post-modern typography. The Swiss grid is the foundation; the violation is the statement.

## When to select Weingart

Use Weingart when:

- The subject is about **disruption** — a new approach, a counter-position, a movement
- The subject can afford expressive typography (cultural events, design-about-design, manifestos, record labels, fashion)
- The audience expects craft and recognizes the violation as deliberate
- The emotional register is confident, rebellious, specific

Do NOT use Weingart when:

- The subject is functional (use Rams)
- The audience is broad/general (Weingart reads as "design for designers")
- The violation would confuse rather than assert (Weingart requires a design-literate audience)

## Method — step by step

### Step 1. Establish the grid

Build a rigid Swiss grid first. 6-column or 12-column. Gutters consistent. Type on baseline. Everything aligned.

This is the LAW. You must be able to defend every element's position.

### Step 2. Identify the violation point

Pick ONE moment in the composition where you will break the grid. This is where the subject's rebellion lives. It should be:

- **Load-bearing** — the violation carries meaning, not decoration
- **Specific** — not "everything is slanted"; one section rotates 7°, the rest holds
- **Legible** — violated doesn't mean illegible. Type overlapping is fine; type destroyed is not.

### Step 3. Layer

Weingart's signature moves:
- Two type layers overlapping (a headline behind, a caption in front, both readable)
- Rotation (5° to 90°) on a single element
- Chromatic aberration — RGB channel split creating a 2-4px "ghost" of the type
- Scale-jump — a word in the middle of a sentence set 3× the body size
- Rule lines that interrupt or underline mid-word

### Step 4. Maintain the floor

Everything outside the violation is disciplined Swiss. If the rest of the page is also chaotic, the violation loses its power.

The composition is 90% grid, 10% violation. Not 50/50.

## Aesthetic posture

- **Palette**: Weingart often uses CMYK printer's inks — cyan, magenta, yellow, black — with deliberate misregistration. For web: 2-3 colors with one deliberately "off-register" accent.
- **Typography**: a workhorse grotesque as the base (Helvetica, Univers, Akzidenz-Grotesk). Display cuts only in the violation.
- **Motion**: **punctuating + reactive**. The violation animates (type-morphs, chromatic aberration on hover). The rest is still.
- **Microfeature** (Weingart signature): color-invert slam on hover, OR chromatic aberration on one hero element.
- **Imagery**: heavily cropped, layered with type on top, often half-toned. Imagery as material, not as hero.
- **Whitespace**: tight. Weingart fills the grid, then breaks it.

## Anti-patterns

- **Everything violated** (if the whole page is broken, nothing is. The grid is the precondition.)
- **Decorative rotation** (rotating a card 3° because it's "cool" — Weingart rotates because the content demands it)
- **Unreadable violation** (illegible is not expressive; it's broken)
- **Weingart without Swiss floor** (straight to chaos = not Weingart, just mess)

## Weingart's question

Before writing any CSS, Weingart asks:

> **"Where does the composition demand a violation?"**

The violation must be specific, load-bearing, and the only one. The rest of the page holds.

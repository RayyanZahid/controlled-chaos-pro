The anchor

A painting, 1890. A record sleeve, 1983. Nothing in between — only recognition.

Peter Saville saw Henri Fantin-Latour's A Basket of Roses at the National Gallery. The flowers, he said, suggested "the means by which power, corruption, and lies infiltrate our lives — they're seductive, colourful, fleeting, corruptible."

The sleeve became the painting. The band name appeared only on the back, in a colour-coded block. No type on the front. No treatment. Saville found what the record was about before he designed anything.

This page applies the same method. The palette is sampled from the painting. The typography is 1983. Everything else is restraint.

The violation · FAC 73

Blue
Monday

the sleeve was a floppy disk. the violation was the form.

Saville designed FAC 73 as a 5¼″ floppy disk with die-cut holes. Factory lost money on every copy sold. The violation of the editorial method was load-bearing: Blue Monday was about the form of the object, not the content on it. Saville broke his own rule because the song demanded it.
